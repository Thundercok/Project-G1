# Project G1 — Story Bible

> *"You have been here before. You will be here again. That is the point."*
> — recovered audio, Speaker unknown, Iteration ████

An original story in the spirit of 1998 — an ordinary engineer, a catastrophic
experiment, a government that executes its witnesses, and a stranger in a suit who
is always, always watching. But underneath the familiar shape is something new:
**this is not the first time it has happened, and you are the only reason it keeps happening.**

---

## Logline

A senior test engineer survives the failure of an experiment meant to hold open a
doorway to *elsewhere* — only to discover the doorway was never to another **place**,
but to every previous **time** the same disaster occurred. He is not a survivor of
the catastrophe. He is its anchor. And the man in the suit is not here to save him,
kill him, or study him. He is here to make sure the disaster never, ever ends.

---

## The One New Idea (read this first)

Everything below hangs on a single twist that separates G1 from its inspirations:

**The Threshold is a temporal aperture, and the Corvus Annex is a loop.**

- Project G1 has been run **many times**. Every catastrophic failure folds the
  facility back to the morning of the test. Reality resets — but not cleanly.
- The **Strays** ("aliens") are not fauna from another world. They are the
  **time-warped remains of the people and creatures from previous failed loops** —
  staff, soldiers, and specimens who never escaped, folded through the aperture and
  degraded across hundreds of iterations into something that only *remembers* being
  human. They are not invading. They are **coming home** — clawing back toward the
  one moment they all still share.
- **Chad Thundercock is the Anchor.** His consciousness is phase-locked to the
  Threshold's resonant frequency. He is the single constant across every iteration.
  The experiment can be *opened* by machinery — but it can only be **stabilized** by
  him being present and alive. Without the Anchor, the loop cannot exist. This is why
  he always survives. This is why he was assigned to the test he was too senior to refuse.
- **The Auditor is an accountant of iterations.** He works for **the Concordance** —
  an inter-iteration bureaucracy that **harvests the energy released by catastrophes
  that never resolve.** A disaster that ends produces one burst of energy. A disaster
  that loops forever is a renewable resource. The Auditor's job is not to kill Chad
  or to help him. It is to make sure Chad keeps *almost* escaping, run after run,
  because a loop that nearly breaks but never does is the most profitable state of all.

The result is a story where the antagonist **wants the player to fail**, and where
the only true victory — collapsing the loop — is also an act of self-erasure.

## Setting

**The Corvus Deep Research Annex** — a subterranean materials-science facility built
into a decommissioned missile-silo complex, deniable and off every map. Sub-Level C
houses **Project G1**: the attempt to hold open a microscopic aperture the physics
team named the **Threshold**.

Officially, Corvus studies exotic materials pulled through the aperture. Unofficially,
Corvus does not know what it truly is. The Annex was **built around the aperture, not
the other way around** — every corridor, generator, and blast door is a resonance
component in a machine the size of a small city, tuned to a frequency no instrument on
site can name. The staff think they are widening a hole. They are winding a clock.

The deeper you descend, the more the architecture stops obeying the blueprints. The
**Undercroft** beneath Sub-Level C is older than the silo — spore-lit, grown-over,
threaded with pods — and it is not a natural cave. It is **the accumulated wreckage of
every previous loop, compacted into one impossible basement.** Corridors that were
never built. Doors with your handprint already worn into them. A save terminal that
greets you by name before you have touched it.

## The Ledger (how the loop shows itself to the player)

The uniqueness has to be *felt*, not just narrated. The loop surfaces through the game's
existing systems — story cards, terminals, CCTV screens, and the Auditor's cameos:

- **Iteration count.** Terminals and the ending card display a redacted iteration
  number (`ITERATION ██7`). It quietly implies a number far larger than "the first time."
- **Your own corpses.** Environmental set-pieces feature dead engineers in the *same
  orange hazard suit with the same Λ emblem* — previous Chads who chose wrong. One is
  slumped at the exact console you're meant to use. His notes are in your handwriting.
- **Graffiti from past runs.** Scrawled on maintenance walls, escalating in tone across
  the campaign: *"DON'T STABILIZE IT" → "THE DOOR GOES BACKWARDS" → "HE COUNTS US" →
  "BREAK THE RING, NOT THE GLASS."* The player slowly realizes these are messages left
  for them, by them.
- **The Taken remember.** Zombified staff ("the Taken") occasionally speak a single
  clear word before attacking — a name, an apology, *"again"* — hinting at the trapped
  human echo inside.
- **The Auditor never fights.** He appears behind sealed glass, atop unreachable walls,
  inside a CCTV feed you're watching from another room — always **writing in a ledger**,
  never hostile, sometimes faintly approving when you take damage. His presence is the
  tell: the closer you get to escape, the more pleased he looks, because the loop is
  about to pay out.

## Factions & Characters

- **Chad Thundercock (the player)** — a senior test engineer in a Class-IV orange
  hazard suit bearing the Λ calibration emblem. Competent, unremarkable, exactly the
  wrong seniority to refuse the morning's test. He does not know he is the Anchor. Over
  three chapters he learns that his survival is not luck — it is *load-bearing*, and
  that everyone in the facility is really fighting over one question: **does Chad step
  through the ring, or not?**

- **The Auditor (villain model)** — a gaunt man in a dark suit with a briefcase,
  appearing in places no one could reach and gone when you look twice. Courteous,
  patient, faintly bored. He is the Concordance's field auditor for the Corvus loop. He
  will open a locked door *for* you at the perfect dramatic moment — not out of mercy,
  but because a Chad who escapes too early doesn't generate enough yield. **He is
  farming the near-miss.**

- **The Sweepers (HECU-style soldiers)** — a military containment unit whose orders are
  witness termination, not evacuation. The twist: **the Sweepers are loop-native.** They
  have run this cleanup hundreds of times. Most are on autopilot, unaware. But a fanatical
  few — the ones with hand-scratched tally marks inside their helmets — *know*, and they
  fight with the terror of men who have done this forever and want it to stop. Their
  radio chatter degrades from professional to prophetic as the campaign proceeds.

- **The Taken (zombies)** — Annex staff changed by what came through. In G1's mythology,
  a Taken is a human whose echo has been **partially folded back from a future loop** —
  a person overwritten by the memory of their own next death. Killing one is a mercy and
  a small collapse of the cycle. This matters at the ending.

- **The Strays (aliens)** — the degraded survivors of prior iterations, returning through
  the aperture. Weaker Strays are near-mindless; the **Elite Alien** and the **Level 3
  guardians** are old — coherent enough to *guard the ring*, because they have learned
  that the Anchor closing the loop is the only thing that can finally release them.

- **The Concordance (unseen)** — never shown directly, only implied through the Auditor's
  ledger, standardized signage that no one at Corvus authored, and the impossible
  bureaucratic tidiness of the Undercroft. They are not evil in a mustache-twirling way.
  They are worse: **indifferent, and extremely well-organized.** To them, Corvus is a
  line item that has performed above expectations for a very long time.

## Timeline / Backstory

1. **Before the loop.** The aperture is discovered during silo decommissioning as a
   faint standing resonance. A materials program is stood up to exploit it. No one
   understands it is a temporal structure.
2. **Iteration 0 — the true failure.** The first widening test triggers a resonance
   cascade. The facility is overrun; nearly everyone dies. In the instant of collapse,
   the aperture — for one microsecond — holds. It holds because one engineer's mind
   happened to match its frequency. Instead of closing, the disaster **loops**.
3. **The Concordance arrives.** Drawn by the energy signature of an unresolved catastrophe,
   the Concordance catalogues Corvus and assigns an Auditor. They do not cause the loop —
   they *sustain* it, nudging each run so it neither escapes nor fully dies.
4. **The iterations.** Run after run, the same morning, the same test, the same failure.
   The Anchor — always Chad — always nearly makes it out. The Strays accumulate. The
   Undercroft grows. The ledger fills.
5. **This run (the game).** Something is different. The messages from past loops have
   survived the reset. The guardians are cooperating instead of swarming. The Auditor is
   nervous for the first time. **This is the iteration where the Anchor might figure it out.**

## Chapters (one per level)

### Chapter One — COLD START *(Level 1, `TestScene`)*
Chad wakes on the locker-room floor of Sub-Level C to the resonance cascade already in
progress — **EXPERIMENT FAILED**. Escape through the lab corridor, control room, and
industrial hall, past the Taken, the first Strays, and Sweepers who open fire on sight.

The chapter *reads* as a straight disaster-escape — but salts the loop in: a dead engineer
in your suit at the first console, an iteration number on a terminal, a save point that
knows your name.
*The Auditor watches from behind the Control Room glass, writing.*

### Chapter Two — QUARANTINE *(Level 2, `Level2`)*
The emergency elevator opens on the surface motor pool at dawn. Sweepers and a gunship
hold the yard, executing survivors. The way out is blocked — the only path is **back down**
through the maintenance shaft into the ancient Undercroft.

Here the truth starts to land. The graffiti escalates. A captured Sweeper radio confesses
the cleanup has happened "more times than the counter goes." The **gunship boss** isn't
just an obstacle — its transponder is broadcasting the ledger's audit signal, and downing
it is the first time the Auditor looks *displeased.*
*The Auditor stands on the perimeter wall, above patrols who cannot see him.*

### Chapter Three — THRESHOLD *(Level 3, `Level3`)*
The Undercroft, grown over with pods and spore light — the compacted wreckage of every
loop. The **Threshold ring** hangs at its center, aliens pouring through. The old
guardian-Strays do not swarm you toward the ring; they **clear the path to it**, because
they finally understand what the Anchor can do.

At the ring, Chad — and the player — face the real choice the whole game has been about:

- **STEP THROUGH (Stabilize).** The intuitive, "heroic escape" option. It resets the loop.
  Chad wakes on the locker-room floor. The disaster begins again. The Concordance is paid.
  *This is the option the Auditor has spent every iteration steering you toward.* Choosing
  it earns his quiet, satisfied nod — and the cruelest ending card.
- **COLLAPSE THE RING (Break).** Turn the crowbar — the first tool of the trade — on the
  resonance emitters instead of stepping through. Shatter the anchor point. The loop
  cannot exist without a stabilizing consciousness present at collapse, and by refusing to
  step through, Chad becomes that collapse. Every trapped echo — every Taken, every Stray,
  every past Chad — is released at once. The Concordance's renewable disaster finally
  *resolves.* But an Anchor that ends the loop is unmade across all iterations: Chad is
  erased from every timeline that ever contained him. **He was never here. He is the only
  one who chose to make that true.**

## Endings

**Ending A — STABILIZED (the loop continues):**
```
STATUS: EXPERIMENT FAILED — FACILITY OVERRIDDEN
GOVERNMENT ORDER: TERMINATE ALL WITNESSES
SUBJECT: CHAD THUNDERCOCK
DISPOSITION: SURVIVED — RE-ANCHORED
ITERATION: ██8   (audit yield: NOMINAL)
The Auditor closes his ledger. Somewhere, a locker-room floor is cold again.
```

**Ending B — COLLAPSED (the loop breaks):**
```
STATUS: THRESHOLD COLLAPSED — CYCLE RESOLVED
CONCORDANCE AUDIT: TERMINATED (asset lost)
SUBJECT: CHAD THUNDERCOCK
DISPOSITION: UNMADE — the door goes backwards, and no one walks out of it
For the first time in ██8 iterations, the locker-room floor stays empty.
The graffiti is gone. So is the hand that wrote it.
```

## Themes & Tone

- **The near-miss as a prison.** The scariest antagonist isn't one that kills you — it's
  one that needs you to *almost* win, forever.
- **Heroism vs. escape.** Every instinct a shooter trains — *reach the exit, step through
  the portal, survive* — is the wrong move here. The game weaponizes its own genre grammar.
- **Cost, not reward.** Half-Life ends with the G-Man handing Gordon a job. G1 ends with
  the Auditor handing Chad a bill — and the only way to refuse it is to stop existing.
- **Tone:** dry, industrial, 1998-cold. No exposition dumps. The truth is assembled from
  corpses, graffiti, degrading radio chatter, iteration counters, and a man in a suit who
  is always writing something down and never explains what.

## How this maps onto what already exists

Nothing above requires new engine systems — it reuses G1's current toolkit:

| Story beat | Existing system |
|---|---|
| Iteration counters, "knows your name" saves | `G1Terminal`, `G1SaveSystem`, `G1Checkpoint` |
| The Auditor's silent cameos | `G1GManCameo`, `G1AuditorCutsceneActor`, `G1CCTVScreen` |
| Graffiti / past-loop messages | `G1StoryCard` (lore cards) + decal materials |
| Taken speaking one word | `G1ZombieAI` + `G1Audio` procedural bark |
| Sweeper radio degrading to prophecy | `G1SoldierBarks` |
| Gunship broadcasting the audit signal | `G1HelicopterBoss` |
| The two endings at the ring | `G1EndingCutscene` + a crowbar-vs-emitter interaction |

The through-line: **the crowbar you start with is the tool that ends the loop.** The first
weapon and the final choice are the same object — which is about as 1998 as it gets.
