# Characters & animation

![Walk cycles](images/protagonist_rig_walk_f1.png)

## The cast

- **Protagonist** — scientist in a hazard-orange protective suit: Λ chest emblem,
  glasses, goatee, Geiger-counter unit with a glowing screen on the right forearm.
- **Villain** — the man in the suit: pale, gaunt, red tie, briefcase in the left
  hand, faint teal glow in the eyes. Idle animation slowly scans the head side to
  side; the briefcase arm stays stiff while walking.

Both are ~1.8 m, ~5.5–6k triangles, flat-shaded.

## Skeleton

Generated by `Tools/blender/rig_character.py`:

```
root
└─ hips ── spine ── chest ── neck ── head
   │            └─ upper_arm.L/R ── forearm.L/R ── hand.L/R
   └─ thigh.L/R ── shin.L/R ── foot.L/R
```

16 deform bones — deliberately minimal, HL1-era budget. `root` sits at the ground
for future root motion.

## Rigid skinning (and why)

Every mesh part is assigned **100% to exactly one bone** (a vertex group created
per part before joining). No blended weights. For characters made of discrete
armor plates and suit panels this is strictly better than auto-weights: joints
read as mechanical articulation and nothing smears or candy-wraps. It is also
exactly how late-90s engines rigged characters, so it reinforces the aesthetic.

The part→bone mapping lives in `bone_for()` in `rig_character.py`. When adding a
new part to a character, either match an existing name prefix (e.g. anything
starting with `hair` goes to the head bone) or add a rule there — the script
raises immediately on any unmapped part, so you can't silently ship an unskinned
piece.

## Animations

Authored as keyframed pose actions in the same script:

| Clip | Length | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| `Idle` | 3 s (protagonist), 4 s (villain) | breathing; villain adds a slow head scan |
| `Walk` | 1 s cycle | contact/passing keys at frames 1/8/16/23/31; arms counter-swing |

Both actions are pushed onto NLA tracks so the FBX exporter writes them as separate
takes. Amplitudes are parameters at the top of the walk section (`A`, `S`, `ARM_A`)
— the villain simply uses smaller numbers plus a stiff briefcase arm.

**Walk speed vs. stride**: `NPCController.speed` defaults to 0.75 m/s to match the
1-second cycle's stride length. If you retune the cycle, retune the speed or feet
will skate.

## Unity side

`G1SceneBuilder.ConfigureCharacterFbx()` sets each FBX to Generic rig, renames the
takes (`ProtagonistRig|Walk` → `Walk`), and marks every clip looping.
`MakeController()` builds an AnimatorController with two states, `Idle` (default)
and `Walk` — no parameters; `NPCController` switches with
`Animator.CrossFade("Walk", …)` by state name.

`NPCController` usage: no waypoints → stands in Idle; two or more waypoints →
patrols them in a loop with Walk. Rotation is `RotateTowards`, movement is straight
`transform.position` — replace with a NavMeshAgent when real AI arrives.
